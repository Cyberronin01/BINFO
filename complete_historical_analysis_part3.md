## INDIA-SPECIFIC EXAMPLES: THE PATTERN IN ACTION

### 1. Aadhaar Biometric Identity System

#### 8-Step Cycle Applied

**Step 1 - Real need:** Identity verification, benefit delivery to poor
**Step 2 - Hard:** Corruption, leakage, ghost beneficiaries
**Step 3 - Convenient solution:** Biometric ID, instant verification
**Step 4 - Cost:** Biometric data, tracking, privacy compromise
**Step 5 - Monitored:** Authentication logs, transaction data
**Step 6 - Mandatory:** Supreme Court 2018 - mandatory for PAN, taxes, subsidies
**Step 7 - Flow controlled:** UIDAI controls access, government exempted from many data rules
**Step 8 - Standardized:** All services converging on Aadhaar
**Step 9 - New need:** Digital India stack, more integrations

#### Mass vs Deciders

**Mass experience:**
- Must provide biometrics
- Authentication required for basic services
- Data retention beyond control
- No alternative for government services

**Decider experience:**
- UIDAI controls system
- Government has broad exemptions (Section 33-35)
- Can change rules
- Access to all authentication data

#### Supreme Court Judgment (2018)

**Upheld** (*Puttaswamy* 4:1 majority):
- Mandatory for: PAN, tax filing, welfare subsidies (Section 7)
- **Constitutional basis:** Article 21 (Right to Privacy)

**Struck down:**
- Bank account linking (non-mandatory)
- Mobile phone linking (non-mandatory)
- Private companies CANNOT demand Aadhaar
- Data retention beyond 6 months (was 5 years)

**Dissent (Justice Chandrachud):**
- Entire project unconstitutional
- Creates surveillance state
- Violates privacy fundamentally

üìå **Mass dependency created before court ruling. "Optional" became necessary structurally.**

### 2. Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA) 2023

#### What Was EXCLUDED (Critical)

Unlike GDPR and earlier Indian drafts:
- **NO general right to data portability** (present in 2018/2019 drafts, removed in final)
- **NO right to be forgotten**

**What was included:**
- Limited portability via "consent managers" (intermediaries)
- Right to access, correct, erase data
- Right to withdraw consent

#### Why This Matters (Control Pattern)

**GDPR Article 20** (EU):
- Right to receive personal data in structured, commonly used, machine-readable format
- Right to transmit data to another controller
- Based on EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

**DPDPA 2023** (India):
- No comprehensive portability
- Consent managers = limited, mediated portability
- Harder to switch providers

üìå **Locking effect: easier to stay than switch**

#### Mass vs Deciders

**Mass:**
- No easy data export
- Switching costs high
- Dependent on platform ecosystems

**Deciders (Government/Companies):**
- Broad exemptions for "legitimate state purposes" (Sections 17-18)
- Data Processing Board independence questioned
- Less constrained by the law they administer

**Constitutional Challenges:**
- Filed in Supreme Court by digital rights groups
- Argue: violates *Puttaswamy* standard (Right to Privacy)
- Concern: facilitates surveillance, not protection
